The Korean War began during the pre-dawn hours of June 25, 1950, and was the first of several conflicts that would define the Cold War era. The hostilities were initiated when 75,000 North Korean soldiers poured over the 38th parallel to invade neighboring South Korea. Their objective was to overpower the ill prepared South Korean Army and then to establish a repressive communist form of government on their neighbors to the south.

The conflict would rage until the morning of July 27, 1953 when delegates from the opposing sides met to sign the cease fire agreement. Even though combat was ended on that day, the armistice was tenuous and the war was not declared over until the last day of January 1955. President Harry Truman never asked Congress to pass a formal declaration of war and even though more than 54,000 American died in defense of South Korea, the hostilities were never designated as anything more than a police action. Thus, the War in Korea never officially began and it never officially ended.

The Forgotten War

More than 33,600 of the 54,000 deaths were inflicted on the field of battle. Those brave Americans died in defense of the South Korean people and to thwart the spread of communism. Another 103,000 American service members were wounded with more than 3,700 captured, starved and tortured. Of the 8,196 who were taken prisoner or declared missing, the whereabouts of over 7,000 remain un-known some six decades following the ceasefire agreement, a document that North Korea has since declared to be invalid.

The Korean conflict is dubbed the Forgotten War and that label reflects our failure to recognize those who partook in that crusade of the 1950s. Even though the Korean campaign was never declared a war, those bloody clashes produced more than 169,000 U. S. casualties.

Many Americans never knew or have forgotten the staggering costs that are evident in the number of lives and limbs that were sacrificed by young Americans in the frigid mountains of Korea. However, the grateful people of South Korea have not forgotten. The Republic of Korea has generously sponsored the Korean War plaza where you now stand. And this plaza is but a token of that nation’s appreciation to the American service personnel who sacrificed so much in defense of that once fledgling democratic republic.


Battle of the Pusan Perimeter

Seoul, the South Korean capital fell on the fourth night following the onslaught and the surprised and outnumbered South Korean forces were thrown into a state of disarray as the communists marched southward. Americans did not engage the invaders until July 5th and during that first encounter, the American units fought below expectations. The assault continued and the battered U. S. and South Korean forces adopted a delaying strategy as they began their retreat towards the port city of Pusan. There, a few miles outside the city, they formed a defensive ring around Koreas’ second largest metropolis, a ring that became known as the Pusan Perimeter

On July 27, a dour faced Gen. Douglas MacArthur visited his top ground commander, Gen. Walton Walker at his field headquarters outside Pusan. The supreme commander was there to assess conditions inside the so-called Pusan Perimeter, the one small area in all of South Korea that had not fallen to the communists. MacArthur was said to have told Gen. Walker, “There will be no Dunkirk in this command. To retire to Pusan would be unacceptable.” MacArthur was determined to hold the invaders outside the defensive perimeter, the shielding ring that protected the vital port facilities at Pusan.

Gen. Walker immediately issued his stand or die order and for the next six weeks, the

North Koreans threw everything they could muster at the American and South Korean forces. The opposing armies fought to a virtual stalemate before Gen. MacArthur, in perhaps the most intuitive strategy of his brilliant career used U. S. Marines to launch an amphibious assault at Inchon, some two hundred miles to the north of Pusan and on the opposite coast of South Korea.

The raid on Inchon caused the North Korean command to realize that MacArthur had implemented an action to cut their supply lines from the north. They understood that they were being trapped and in danger of being annihilated by Gen. Walkers 8th Army as it raced north from Pusan, and the Marine Divisions moving quickly to the south and east following the landings at Inchon. The sobering reality of MacArthur’s strategy sent the invaders fleeing back towards the 38th Parallel with the 8th Army in hot pursuit. However, Gen. Walker was not allowed to chase the fleeing communists into North Korea. Then, near the end of September, Gen. MacArthur was given the nod to cross the 38th Parallel. The chase was resumed and ended some weeks later at the Yalu River, the boundary between China and North Korea.

The Battle at the Chosin Reservoir

Among the more brutal engagements of the Korean conflict was the struggle at the

Chosin Reservoir, a battle that erupted during late November and early December of 1950. United Nations forces had driven right up to but had stopped on the Korean side of the frozen river boundary. Thus, some five months following the invasion of South Korea the North Koreans were demoralized and defeated. Overwhelming numbers of Chinese soldiers then crossed the icy boundary to join the war on the side of their communist puppets in North Korea.

A detachment of Marines was holding the Chosin Reservoir which was only a few miles inland from the border along the Yalu but the Chinese were determined to seize that electric power producing facility. The outnumbered Marines fought courageously against the onslaught in temperatures that reached minus 54 degrees Fahrenheit. Nonetheless their numbers were inadequate to hold the big reservoir and they began a grueling retreat to the coast. There, they would board U. S. transport ships to be evacuated and saved to fight another day. One Marine who survived the Battle of the Chosin designed a bumper sticker that read: --- “Once Upon a Time Hell Froze Over. We Were There.”

A significant name at the Battle of the Chosin Reservoir is that of Atlanta’s own General Raymond Davis, now deceased. Then, a Lt. Colonel, the future Gen. Davis was dispatched to the Chosin to bring the Marines back to the coast in an orderly retreat. For bravery and heroism during that mission, General Davis was awarded the Medal of Honor.
