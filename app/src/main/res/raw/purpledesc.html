There is a fascinating history behind the medal that is named the Purple Heart. Since April 5, 1917, the medal has been awarded in the name of the President of the United States to those wounded or killed while serving with the U. S. military. The Purple Heart differs though from all other service awards in that it requires no recommendation from anyone. It is a medal of entitlement that is awarded to those who meet a qualifying list of specific criteria. Among the criteria is that the Purple Heart is bestowed for “Being wounded or killed in any action against an enemy of the United States or as a result of an act of any such enemy or opposing armed forces.”

The Purple Heart design experienced several makeovers leading up to its current configuration which was adopted in the 1930s. And the first recipient was none other than Gen. Douglas MacArthur who received the award on February 22, 1932, a date that intentionally coincided with the birth-date of Gen. George Washington who had personally bestowed the original Badge of Military Merit medals on three Revolutionary War soldiers. And from the original Badge of Military Merit awards that were bestowed on that trio of soldiers evolved the current day version of the Purple Heart. In further tribute to our nations’ founding father, an obverse profile of Gen. George Washington defines the medal received by Gen. MacArthur and all Purple Heart medals that have been bequeathed since 1932.

History of the Purple Heart

The history of the Purple Heart is a bit convoluted but it essentially sprang from the Badge of Military Merit that was awarded to those first three recipients by Gen. Washington in August 1782. Thus, the Purple Heart is the longest tenured medal in the pantheon of military service awards that are bestowed by the United States. No one desires or seeks to receive a Purple Heart but it is among the most widely distributed U. S. Military medals. Exact numbers are unknown but it is estimated that more than (1.9) million service members have received the Purple Heart, the medal that reflects our nations gratitude to those brave men and women who became casualties on the field of battle.


Medal Awards Trivia

Pre-dating the Badge of Military Merit was the ---- Fidelity Medallion or “Andre Capture Medal.” --- That medal was awarded to Privates John Paulding, David Williams and Isaac Van Wort, all of the New York State Militia. Those three privates were credited with the capture of Major John Andre, a British army officer who was hanged as a spy during the American Revolutionary War. ----- Andre was responsible for meeting with the traitor, Benedict Arnold, on September 23, 1780 and persuading Arnold to surrender the fort at West Point. As an award for the trio of heroic privates, each was given a $200 yearly pension and a silver medal, the Fidelity Medallion. All were later honored by having counties in Ohio named for them. Also of interest is that Georgia’s own Paulding County was named in recognition of Private John Paulding.


The Badge of Military Merit, created August 7, 1782, seems also to have been awarded only three times. The recipients, Sergeants William Brown --- Elijah Churchill and Daniel Bissell all received the awards personally from Gen. George Washington. --- At the time of the American Revolutionary War, the practice in Europe was to honor high-ranking officers who had achieved victory, rather than the common soldier. But in America, as General Washington said, the “road to glory in a patriot army and a free country is --- open to all.”
