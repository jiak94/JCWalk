Vietnam was the longest war in which the United States was involved prior to the conflict in Afghanistan. However, in much the same manner that the 38th Parallel divided North Korea from South Korea, the 17th Parallel divided the people of North Vietnam from those living in South Vietnam. Thus, for the second time within a decade, the U. S. was to become involved in defending the free citizens in the southern half of a riven nation.

There are many obvious similarities manifest in the conflicts in both Korea and Vietnam. However, the manifold similarities between the war in Korea and the very long war in Vietnam are consistent, even though paradoxical.

In Korea, the war was fought in rugged mountain terrain where below zero temperatures were common and frostbite was a painful reality. In Vietnam, the skirmishes were fought in humid and oppressively hot rice paddies, steamy jungles and muddy rivers.

In Korea, the fight pitted opposing armies on the traditional fields of battle. In Vietnam, the Viet Cong would set booby traps or conduct hit and run ambushes near the scattered villages. They would then disappear into a labyrinth of uncharted tunnels or retreat back into the jungles.

In Korea, planes, jeeps, tanks and armored troop carriers were utilized fully and the role of the helicopter was minor. In Vietnam helicopters did the preponderance of the heavy lifting!

In South Korea the line was held and democracy continues to flourish! Not so in South Vietnam where the line was breached and the democratic government toppled.

In Korea, the U. S. deploys tens of thousands of America’s peacekeeping troops to ensure the truce.

But, there are no American peacekeepers in South Vietnam which no longer exists.

In 1950, the U. S. reacted immediately to repel aggression by North Korea. In Vietnam, no American troops were deployed until the war had raged for more than a decade. Even then, it was only after the French defeat at Dien Bien Phu and their subsequent withdrawal from French Indochina that the U. S. began filling the void in the old Colonial Empire once ruled by France.

The United States Provides Military Advisors

The complexities of U. S. involvement in Vietnam are many! And there are significant political ramifications, half-truths, ill-considered military decisions and other negatives that beg consideration when pondering our nations’ political and military roles in that conflict. From being surprised by the Viet Cong Tet Offensive in January of 1968, --- to the My Lai execution type slayings of Vietnamese villagers some six weeks hence, --- and on to the killing of anti-war students at Kent State University in May of 1970, the U. S. made numerous and critical mistakes both at home and Vietnam in the conduct of that most complex war.

However, the initial U. S. involvement in Vietnam came in 1959 when President Dwight D. Eisenhower dispatched a few hundred advisors to help train the South Vietnamese Army during the presidency of Ngo Dinh Diem. The Diem government was incompetent and he was murdered during a 1963 coup that was alleged to have been plotted by the U. S. during the presidency of John F. Kennedy.

As fighting escalated between Ho Chi Minh’s Viet Cong and the South Vietnamese following the assassination of Ngo Dinh Diem, the number of American advisors was steadily increased until August of 1964. Then, the Gulf of Tonkin incident permanently altered the United States strategy of minimal involvement in Vietnam. And, by 1969, the U. S. had deployed an armed contingent of over 543,000 soldiers, sailors, airmen and Marines to fight a guerrilla force of some 35,000 men who were adept at hit-and- run jungle warfare.

During the Gulf of Tonkin incident, it is asserted that a pair of U. S Destroyers was fired on by North Vietnamese forces as those ships patrolled outside Vietnam’s territorial waters in that Gulf along the coast of Vietnam. Congress quickly passed the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution, an action that gave President Lyndon B. Johnson the authority to escalate the conflict. Then, in March of 1965, President Johnson ordered the first American combat units into Vietnam.

A War with no Purpose

There was no stated purpose for the Vietnam War, a war that did not suddenly erupt as had WW-II and Korea. Vietnam simply evolved over a span of years to become a war that would last for more than a decade. The Vietnam commitment was extremely unpopular with many Americans and thousands of young men refused to be drafted. Many fled to Canada and other nations to avoid service in the military or being sent to prison. Nonetheless, and despite riotous protests and other manifestations of conflict over the war, millions of young Americans disregarded the Counter Culture movement of the 1960s and answered the call to serve. Thus, our debt of gratitude to those who put their lives on the line in Vietnam will endure forever.

Casualties

Nearly three and a half million Americans served in Southeast Asia during the Vietnam era. And, during those 13 years of involvement, a period that includes an eight year span of active combat that began in 1965, more than 58,000 Americans were killed. Of that number, some 47,434 died while engaged in the many battles. In addition, there were more than 153,300 American service-members who were wounded. And, due to the effects of Agent Orange, a chemical used to defoliate enemy hiding places in the dense jungles, thousands of veterans returned home to suffer the effects of a Post-Traumatic Stress condition that will plague them throughout the rest of their lives.

Listed among the more painful casualties of the war were the Americans who were imprisoned in the so-called Hanoi Hilton, one of the more brutal prison camps in history. And it was with joyous relief that millions of Americans saw those POWs exit the planes that brought them to the Philippines. One can never forget the sudden release of pent-up emotions when Admiral Jeremiah Denton, the first POW to step from the first plane, tearfully saluted the flag and uttered the phrase, “God Bless America?”

Bravery and Valor

Bravery and valor were as common in Vietnam as had been those characteristics in previous wars. And among those cited for such gallantry is Georgia’s own Maj. Gen. James E. Livingston, USMC (Ret.). Gen. Livingston, as a young captain and company commander earned the Medal of Honor for his heroic leadership during the clash of forces at Dong Ha and Dai Do, a bloody battle that was fought in April and May of 1968. Gen. Livingston, now living in S. C., was the featured speaker at the founding of the Johns Creek Veterans Association. His book, Noble Warrior, is a must read for those interested in the history of the Vietnam War.

Belated Recognition

Recognition of the men and women who fought in Vietnam was a long time coming! But in May of 2012, President Barak Obama endeavored to rectify that omission by signing a proclamation commemorating the 50Th Anniversary of the Vietnam War. The commemoration will be kept annually until November 11, 2025. And during those 13 years, which symbolize our 13 years in Vietnam, the nation will, on that date, remember the patriotic Americans who served during that divisive time in our nation’s history, a time when those in uniform were often scorned and spat upon. Thankfully, a reflective nation now attempts to rectify that injustice!
