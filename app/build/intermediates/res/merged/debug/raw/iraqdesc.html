The Iraq War, known to many as the Second Gulf War is the campaign that toppled Saddam Hussein from power. The reasons for the conflict are complicated and the final outcome will remain unknown for decades. Nonetheless, it was believed by International Intelligence sources, Military experts and most Elected officials that the struggle would be over quickly. Those, however, were inaccurate assessments and the War in Iraq became expensive, deadly, strategically flawed and politically divisive. During those 8 years of conflict, some 4,491 American lives were lost and more than 3,500 of those deaths were combat related. And we must add to that number of fatalities an additional 32,200 Americans who were wounded.

The Iraqi conflict was the indirect result of America’s outrage over the cruelty that was perpetrated by Islamic terrorists on 9/11, 2001. During that exploitation of evil more than 3,300 individuals succumbed to the flames that engulfed the World Trade Center, -- or to other acts of abhorrent violence on that day in September.

Another of the deadly feats of 9/11 was the smashup of a hijacked passenger plane near Shanksville Pennsylvania. And, except for the courage shown by heroic passengers aboard that flight, that plane would have crashed into either the White House or the Nations’ Capitol. Tragically, another of the four terrorist abducted planes could not be diverted and it went crashing into the Pentagon.

Those nineteen Al Qaeda fanatics were successful in killing thousands of innocent people, creating widespread panic, and damaging our nations’ financial infrastructure with the demolition of the World Trade Center. And, the bin Laden followers were to cause extensive destruction to our Pentagon building, the command post from which America’s five military branches receive their orders.

The heroism validated in the skies over Shanksville foiled the indiscriminate massacre of U. S. Senators and House Members, and even the president was perhaps among the intended targets. The atrocity of 9/11 was a sweeping three-part conspiracy that was designed to weaken the U. S. economy, kill government officials, and disrupt our military command structure.

To Honor the Non Military Heroes

The various Plazas at the Veterans Memorial are dedicated to those who served our nation militarily. However, we cannot let pass the opportunity to verbally salute the heroic actions of those men aboard that plane in Pennsylvania. And we must never forget the 343 firemen and 72 police officers who gave their lives responding to the call for help during the madness of 9/11.

Saddam Hussein’s Defiant Attitude

Saddam’s brazen nature and his bellicose utterances caused many on Capitol Hill and among America’s allies to believe that he stood responsible for 9/11. And he was thought to own stockpiles of weapons of mass destruction, armaments he had pledged to destroy following the first Gulf War in the 1990s. Saddam later reneged on his pledge to provide verifiable proof that he had destroyed those arsenals. And he defiantly prohibited the U. N. sanctioned Inspectors of the opportunity to conduct the agreed upon searches for such weapons.

Even though Iraq’s weapons of mass destruction were never to be located either before, during or subsequent to the eight years of warfare, Saddam’s truculent refusal to honor the terms imposed following Iraq’s incursion into Kuwait caused Intelligence sources to believe that he was at least partially responsible for 9/11. And because of those mistaken conclusions that linked Iraq’s violation of its Kuwaiti neighbor some ten years prior with the terrorist assaults on 9/11, the U. S. and her allies went to war against Iraq in the early weeks of 2003.

Via the transparency of hindsight it seems that Saddam had nothing to do with 9/11. Thus, the War that stemmed from erroneous Intelligence reckonings became a conflict that turned costly, deadly and divisive. The U. S. spent over a trillion dollars fighting the war in Iraq. However, of far greater consequence is the lives’ lost and the debilitating injuries suffered by American service members. And in addition to the American casualties, it is estimated that more than a million Iraqi soldiers and civilians were slain during that conflict.

Weapons of War

Weapons systems continued to evolve during the wars in Afghanistan and Iraq. And in the many dusty villages of those ancient nations, American service members were exposed to the horrors of Incendiary Explosive Devices. --- The (IED) is a simple though deadly weapon that is easily improvised, assembled and detonated by an individual who can be more than a mile away from the buried explosive. And the IED has become the preferred weapon of opportunity in such conflicts. It is a weapon that has inflicted untold pain and suffering on the men and women who have died from or have been mutilated by IEDs. The IED is a deadly mixture of explosives and shrapnel that is readily available to those who engage in guerilla warfare. And the IED is liable for many thousands of lost American lives, limbs, eyes and a host of debilitating injuries.

Another weapon used extensively in the Afghan Iraqi conflicts is the RPG or the Rocket Propelled Grenade, a potent antitank weapon. The RPG is launched from a human shoulder in much the same manner that the bazooka was used in WW-II and the toll taken on tanks, trucks and soldiers engaged in combat is devastating.

The unmanned drone is also a new and sophisticated weapon that has come into wide-

spread use in Afghanistan and Iraq. Drones are employed to perform feats that once required piloted planes to fly into deadly enemy fire over certain targets. The mission of the drone is to reduce the incidents that place American lives in danger, and they will continue to be improved technologically. The life-saving role of the drone will be expanded and the use of unmanned drones will become more and more common in wars that are yet to be fought.

Conclusion

The reasons for the Iraqi War will never be totally understood and the results can never be fully measured. However even though the war did achieve its purpose of toppling Saddam Hussein from power, he almost certainly had nothing to do with 9/11. Nonetheless, the events of 9/11 became the indirect reason for the war against him.

There are striking similarities in the reasons for and the timelines that define the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan. Thus, to avoid redundancy in the narratives of those conflicts, this segment has delved primarily into the happenings of 9/11. And the series of provocative events preceding the wars in those two nations is narrated at the Afghan Plaza.
