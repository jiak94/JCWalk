North Africa and Europe

The events leading up to the Second World War are complex. However, WW-II was to become the most devastating struggle in the annals of warfare. There was more money spent, more property damaged, and more societal changes recorded during that war than from any previous or subsequent clash in the history of human conflict.

More appalling though was the number of human-beings massacred. Men, women and children died by the millions from the incidents of warfare and the brutality imposed upon subjugated peoples by Nazi Germans and Feudal Minded Japanese. WW-II was a time when our western concepts of democracy hung by a thread. And, except for the men and women of the Greatest Generation stepping forth to put an end to the madness of ruthless dictators, the world would have been plundered by tyrants.

It is estimated that between fifty and seventy million casualties were suffered during the Second World War. And that a staggering 80% of those killed or wounded were from the four countries of Russia, Germany, China and Poland.

Some forty-six million mortalities are among the estimated seventy million casualties and the Soviet Union documented more than 21-million of those killed. Amazingly, some 57%, over twenty-seven million of the wartime deaths were suffered by civilians. And even more ominous is that more than half of the civilian deaths were visited on women and children.

Ironically, the Spanish flu pandemic of WW-I killed over 100 million individuals, a number that is more than double the fatalities inflicted during the Second World War. In fact, the total deaths suffered during both world wars was tens of millions fewer than the number of victims who succumbed to the silent but even more lethal pandemic of the Spanish flu.

Battle of Stalingrad

It is estimated that the Battle of Stalingrad produced casualties of at least 800,000 with perhaps as many as 1.6 million soldiers and civilians listed among the dead, wounded or missing.

That deadly contest produced the largest number of casualties ever from a single battle. And Russia, for a second time in its poignant history, literally became a human slaughter-house!

U. S. Casualties

By comparison, the 400,000 deaths suffered by U. S. forces during all of WW-II was a merciful number. Nonetheless, the visceral agony visited upon those thousands of innocent American families is a major national tragedy. Those gold-star relatives suffered the grief of war in much the same manner as did their Old World counterparts who were personally subjected to artillery fire and the countless tons of deadly explosives that were dropped from the skies on their homes and cities.

The North African Campaign

U. S. forces saw their first WW-II action against Nazi soldiers in November of 1942 during the fighting in North Africa. The struggle between British and Italian rivals had raged since June of 1940. And, following a series of “tit for tat” engagements that resulted in the destruction of the Italian Army in Libya, Hitler dispatched Gen. Erwin Rommel and his Afrika Korps to salvage and reinforce the defeated divisions of Mussolini.

North Africa then became a wartime testing laboratory for an amazing triad of innovative ground-war commanders. Germany’s Erwin Rommel, dubbed the Desert Fox, would hone his substantial skills against those of Old Blood and Guts, the U. S. Army’s George S. Patton, as well as those of Great Britain’s highly cautious but effective Bernard L. Montgomery. Those three generals, each of them competent in diverse methods of ground warfare, were seemingly ordained to engrave their names on the pages of world history. The Allied commanders, Patton and Montgomery, detested each other! But there was a respectful love-hate relationship between Patton and his German adversary, Erwin Rommel.

Contentious Issues

There were contentious issues between the Vichy French and the Allies in North Africa. A situation that became particularly tense in matters pertaining to the French fleet that had not surrendered when the French government was forced to capitulate. However, that topic is complex and cannot be addressed in this brief narration. It is though an interesting chapter on the war in North Africa. And now, many decades removed from those historic struggles in the deserts of North Africa are geographic names that again make the news in the 21st Century. They are Benghazi, Tripoli, Tobruk, Libya, Egypt, Algiers, Tunisia and El Alamein.

The first hostile action involving American troops was at the Kasserine Pass in Tunisia, a battle in which previously untested American units were routed by the Germans. However, those divisions would regroup under the newly appointed Gen. George S. Patton. And, under his command, they would within days fight back and drive the Nazi regiments from that strategic pass in the endless wastes of North Africa.

North Africa Casualties

British Commonwealth units suffered over 220,000 battle casualties with more than 35,000 of those troops confirmed as dead. Germany saw some 130,000 of its soldiers captured but the Italians, whose hearts were not in the fight lost over 340,000 soldiers that were taken prisoner. U. S. casualties totaled more than18, 000, with some 2,715 conformed dead. Material losses were also enormous in North Africa. From among the warring factions over 8,000 air-craft, 6,200 guns, 2,500 tanks and 70,000 vehicles were destroyed or rendered useless.

Sicily and Italy

Following the Allied victory in Africa in May of 1943, the next fighting began with the invasion of Sicily during the pre-dawn hours of July10. On that morning, more than 150,000 British and American troops, supported by over 2,500 ships, gliders and landing vessels launched their amphibious assault on Sicily. American forces were under the command of Gen. Dwight Eisenhower and British Commonwealth forces were commanded by Gen. Harold Alexander.

The Allies were successful in driving the German and Italian divisions from Sicily’s coastal cities. However, due to the rivalry between Generals Patton and Montgomery, tactical mistakes were made by both men. Those missteps are believed by some to have been responsible for the escape of thousands of Axis troops, troops that would go on to fight another day against Gen. Mark Clarkes’ 5th Army as it battled its way up the boot of Italy.

As was true in North Africa, there are far too many details to be covered in this brief narration of the events in Sicily. However, among the noteworthy happenings were:

1. The battles in western Sicily brought two predominantly Roman Catholic armies into direct conflict for the first time ever. Patton’s 7th Army, comprised largely of Italian-Americans who were Roman Catholic, was opposed by Italian forces that were almost entirely of that religion.

2. In cooperation with the Sicilian Mafioso, the Allied invaders were enabled to quickly appoint

the leaders needed to run the affairs of the conquered cities.

3. Gen. Patton indulged his ornery legend during the campaign in Sicily via the field hospital slapping incidents involving soldiers suffering from battle fatigue.

4. Gen. Patton also sated his fiery nature when he pulled out a pearl handled pistol and shot a Sicilian farmers’ stubborn donkey that was hindering his troops from crossing a bridge.

5. Many Italian-American soldiers had relatives living on Sicily and instead of fearing the invading American forces; large numbers of Sicilians greeted them as friends or family.

D-Day – June 6, 1944

The day finally arrived on June 6, 1944 when the British retreat from the French port of Dunkirk would be avenged with the D-Day landings at Normandy. The amphibious assault, the largest such invasion in history followed a series of nighttime parachute drops behind heavily fortified German installations along the rugged coast in the north of France. Casualties were enormous and German units fought desperately to repel the landings. However, British and American forces fought tenaciously to secure footholds on beaches with code names like Utah and Omaha, And Operation Overlord, the largest air, land and sea invasion in history involved some 150,000 men, 5,000 ships and 11,000 airplanes.

Most of the men were not yet twenty one when they waded thru the blood stained surf carrying backpacks holding over 80 lbs. of equipment. The water was often over their heads and once ashore, they had to race 200 yards across open beach to reach the cover of the vertical cliffs from which German soldiers were firing furiously. --- One wag said that, “Blanketed by small-arms fire and bracketed by artillery, they found themselves in hell.” Nonetheless, and despite the10, 000 D-Day casualties that saw over 4,000 Allied warriors lying dead on the beaches, Fortress Europe had been effectively penetrated.

Battle of the Bulge

By December the Allied armies had fought their way across France and were nearing the German border. Then, on December 16, 1944, some six months following D-Day, the retreating Nazis’ launched a counter offensive in the snow covered Ardennes Forests of Luxembourg and Belgium. Some of the most intense fighting of WW-II occurred in those dense woods and the frigid temperatures dipped below zero. However, before the deadly skirmishing was over, the Battle of the Bulge had exacted in excess of 75,000 American casualties. Of that number, 8,400 were killed, 46,000 wounded, and an additional 21,000 were listed as captured or missing. The U. S. divisions also lost over 800 tanks.

German casualties were substantially heavier with more than 100,000 Nazi soldiers numbered among those killed, wounded or missing. And more than six hundred of their dwindling supply of tanks was destroyed. ------ The Battle of the Bulge was Germany’s final effort to foil the inevitable and following the forty day delay of Allied progress caused by that battle, the American and British forces regrouped and resumed their march to victory.

Gen. Simpsons’ 9th Army

Gen. William Simpsons 9th Army quickly reached a position on the Elbe River some forty miles northwest of Berlin and could have easily taken the German capitol. However, Simpson was ordered not to advance beyond the Elbe waterway and the Russians were allowed to be the first of the Allied armies to enter the German capitol. And it was only after the Red Army had arrived in Berlin that the Nazis’ agreed to the terms of unconditional surrender in May of 1945.

The Japanese-American 442nd Regiment and the Tuskegee Airmen

No history of WW-II would be complete without an acknowledgement of the role played by the 442 Regiment, an all Japanese-American infantry unit that fought heroically in Europe and later in Korea. For their action during WW-II, primarily in the south of France, Germany and Italy, the 442nd would become the most decorated infantry unit in the history of the United States Army. The Regiment was awarded eight Presidential Unit Citations and twenty one of their men earned the Medal of Honor. And while the 3,800 men of the 442nd Regiment fought gallantly against the Nazi enemy in Europe, over 110,000 of their Japanese-American friends and relatives were incarcerated in prison camps that were scattered from the Mississippi River to the West Coast.

The Tuskegee Airmen

The Tuskegee Airmen is a designation for the African-American men who piloted and manned specific squadrons of bombers and pursuit planes during WW-II. Tuskegee fighter units protected the mammoth squadrons of American bombers as they deluged destruction down on the cities and factories of Germany and other Axis nations. And those bomber crews were always pleased when one or more of the Tuskegee fighter groups were assigned to protect them as they flew over enemy targets.

Tuskegee pilots flew in close proximity to their assigned squadrons and losses among the big planes under their protection was approximately half those lost by other fighter units. Their strategy of providing close security for the lumbering bombers prohibited Tuskegee pilots from pursuing and shooting down as many enemy planes as did their counterparts. However, because of their strategy of providing close protection for their assigned squadrons of heavy bombers, the Tuskegee pilots and airmen are responsible for saving the lives of many bomber crews during raids over Europe.

The Holocaust

One of the more moving human tragedies of all times occurred during WW-II. Millions of innocent Jewish citizens from nations across the breadth of Europe were herded into filthy freight cars and forcefully transported to suffer the horrors of concentration camps with names like Auschwitz, Dachau, Sobibor, Treblinka and Buchenwald. There, they were separated from families, starved, tortured and sent to their deaths in the cruel gas chambers of Nazi Germany.

The WW-II Holocaust was indeed one of the darkest moments in recorded history! And, to paraphrase and quote a line in a speech by President Franklin D. Roosevelt following the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor, The Holocaust represents a time, a place and an era that will Live in Infamy!

Pacific Theater of Operations

The date was December 7, 1941 when bombs fell from the skies over the Hawaiian island of Oahu to decimate the American fleet anchored at Pearl Harbor. That unprovoked attack by the Imperial forces of Japan plunged the United States into a war that had ravaged most of Europe since 1939. And those Old World skirmishes were but the opening salvos in what would become the Second World War, - the conflict that would be defined as the most deadly struggle in human history.

On that balmy Sunday in the Islands, over 3,500 casualties were recorded with dozens of civilians among the dead and wounded. The Navy lost a total of eighteen ships and the armed services lost a mixed bag of 263 warplanes. Of the eight battleships in port, four were sunk, three damaged, and one grounded. Even though six of those ships would be returned to engage in combat, they were out of action for some critical months as the war raged across the oceans and onto the far flung islands of the South Seas. In addition to the huge battle wagons, three combat ready light cruisers and an equal number of vital destroyers were lost to the Pacific Command.

Immediately following the Surprise Attack

On December 8, President Franklin D. Roosevelt addressed a joint session of the U. S. Congress with a stirring six minute speech in which he labelled December 7, 1941, as: “a date that will live in infamy.” Within an hour of the president’s speech, and with but one dissenting vote, Congress declared that a state of war existed between the United States and Japan, and the President was instantly empowered to wage war with all of the country’s resources. Four days later, Germany and Italy declared war on the United States. Congress then voted unanimously to go to war against those Axis nations that were led by Hitler and Mussolini.

Providential Intervention

The Pacific Fleet’s aircraft carriers, delayed by clusters of squalls while returning to port following a series of drills in the Pacific were not in port on that ill-fated Sunday. Had either of those big carriers or their squadrons of fighter planes, or any escorting destroyers been lost in the Japanese attack, the war in the Pacific Theater of Operations could have easily taken a different turn, a turn that could have negated the eventual outcome.

Whether it was Fate or a Divine Providence that kept those aircraft carriers at sea, a number of implausible events materialized on that long ago Sunday morning at Pearl Harbor. The turbulent seas that kept the carriers out of port and the Japanese failure to destroy the fuel tanks and dry docks, facilities that would be so vital to the war effort, shaped the mysterious chain of events that permitted the United States to recoup and go quickly on the offensive.

The first of the retaliatory actions occurred on April 18, 1942. On that date, then Lt. Col. Jimmy Doolittle led his all-volunteer crews on an historic raid on Japan. For the first time ever, sixteen land-based B-25 Mitchell bombers were dispatched from the deck of an aircraft carrier, the USS Hornet, to rain down bombs of destruction on Tokyo and other strategic targets on Honshu Island. The Doolittle Raiders suffered debilitating losses! But their bold action validated that the United States could inflict anguish and destruction deep inside the culturally isolated nation that called itself, the “Land of the Rising Sun.”

Noteworthy Battles - Pacific Theater

Among the noteworthy battles in the Pacific Theater was the naval Battle at Midway, an action that ensued during the first week in June 1942. The crucial victory at Midway changed the direction of the war in favor of the United States. Ironically, the battle in the waters off that desolate island of such strategic value pitted three of the U. S. aircraft carriers that were absent at Pearl Harbor, against four of the six Japanese carriers that were involved in that attack. The battle raged into a fourth day but when the smoke finally dissipated on June 7, some six months following Pearl Harbor, Japanese forces had lost four aircraft carriers, a heavy cruiser and scores of fighter planes. In contrast, U. S. losses at Midway consisted of one aircraft carrier, the USS Yorktown, a destroyer and a much smaller number of carrier based war planes.

Battle of Guadalcanal

Following Midway, United States forces initiated the first of many ground assaults against Japanese held island targets as they invaded Guadalcanal in the Solomon Islands. The Imperial Army resisted furiously from August 1942 through February of 1943 and both sides endured heavy losses. However, due to men like Gunnery Sergeant John Basilone, a first generation Italian-American from the little town of Raritan New Jersey, U. S. forces fought and won the fight for control of that steamy hellhole in the south Pacific.

John Basilone received the Medal of Honor for valor at Guadalcanal. He was then brought home to pitch the sale of war bonds. Basilone loathed the assignment and wanted nothing more than to be back with his Marines. After repeated rejections of his request to return to his unit, he appealed directly to President Roosevelt. His wish was granted and Sgt. John Basilone returned to action. Some months following, and during the very first day of fighting at Iwo Jima, Sgt. Basilone died a hero. ---- For extraordinary heroism during his final hours, he was posthumously awarded the Navy Cross, the second highest honor that can be bestowed on sailors and Marines. It should be noted that Basilone also earned the distinction of being the only enlisted Marine to receive both the Medal of Honor and Navy Cross during WW-II. This heroic son of Italian immigrants was indeed a member of America’s Greatest Generation! His story is a tribute to the many unheralded veterans who held high the flag during WW-II and subsequent conflicts.

Tarawa

Following Guadalcanal, American forces continued their island hopping strategy of using amphibious assaults and their flame throwing brand of warfare to engage the Japanese as they hop-skipped across the Pacific while pushing ever closer to Japan. During the battle for Tarawa, a strategic atoll in the Gilbert Islands, Japanese and American forces suffered a combined total of 6,400 killed in just four days of fighting.

Iwo Jima

At Iwo Jima, a campaign made famous by the photo of five Marines and a Navy Corpsman planting the flag atop Mt. Surabachi, casualties were enormous! The U. S. deployed over 70,000 men at Iwo Jima. – And, from that number, over 6,800 were killed and another 19,000 wounded. The Japanese saw 19,000 of their 22,000 soldiers killed during the fighting, and the death rate was an astonishing 86%. Just 216 of their troops submitted to being captured and the remaining 3,000 Japanese defenders went into hiding. The Imperial soldiers were tenacious fighter and fragments of the Shogun culture were buried deeply within their hearts and minds. They thought it a dishonor to surrender and they fought fanatically until the bitter end.

The Bloody Campaign at Okinawa

The Battle of Okinawa followed Iwo Jima as Allied forces nudged relentlessly nearer to Japan in the early months of 1945. Okinawa lay just 340 miles from the home islands and it was to be used as the kick off point for the invasion of the islands that comprise the nation of Japan. However, with the dropping of the A- Bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki, the invasion of the home-land never became necessary. Okinawa was, however, vital to the expected invasion and it was the largest amphibious assault in the Pacific Theater. The battle raged for eighty two days and both sides suffered tremendously from the loss of ships, planes and military personnel.

Kamikaze Warfare Employed at Okinawa

The Kamikaze pilots, who took off from Japan with the knowledge that theirs was strictly a one way mission, were fearless in their attacks on the predominantly U. S. fleet of warships covering the invasion at Okinawa. The Kamikazes inflicted heavy losses on the vast armada of vessels and many ships were lost. However, the intensity of return fire from U. S. destroyers patrolling the defensive perimeter off Okinawa inflicted debilitating losses on the Kamikazes.

The Kamikaze pilots would launch their torpedoes at a targeted vessel and then attempt to crash their planes into a second ship. If the insane crash could be accomplished, then one Kamikaze pilot stood responsible for killing hundreds of soldiers, sailors and Marines while sinking or damaging two American warships.

Japan lost some 77,000 soldiers who were killed or committed suicide at Okinawa, the goriest battle in the Pacific. And U. S. forces endured over 14,000 deaths from among a total of 65,000 casualties. And, up to 150,000 native Okinawans committed suicide rather than submit to living under the imagined cruelty that would be imposed by the United States.

Germany had capitulated during May of 1945 and the war in the Pacific would end once the A-Bombs were employed in August. Thus, the world breathed a sigh of relief when on September 2, 1945, a Japanese delegation boarded the battleship USS Missouri to formally sign the document of unconditional surrender. And, with the execution of that document the bloodiest war in world history came to a merciful conclusion.

Atlanta resident, Jim Starnes, then a Navy Lt. Commander and Chief Navigation Officer aboard the USS Missouri, per Navy protocol, was the Officer of the Deck who permitted the Japanese delegation to come aboard and then send them forward to the bow where Gen. MacArthur, Adm. Nimitz and an Allied delegation of Admirals and Generals awaited them.
