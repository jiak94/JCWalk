Throughout history women have gone to war and placed themselves in harms’ way for various and multiple reasons. From the legendary feats of ancient Amazon warriors to the conflicts in Iraq and Afghanistan, and progressing on to the wars of the future, women have served and will serve beside their male counterparts wherever there are battles to be fought.

Among the most celebrated female combatants of history is the French born, peasant heroine, Joan of Arc. Joan was born in 1412 and during the time when the so-called 100 Years War raged between France and England. And she was but 17 when King Charles VII sent her to Orleans as the captain of a troop of men. Joan guided her troop to victory during that battle in 1429 and it was because of her actions at Orleans that she was immortalized in the chronicles of world history. Joan was subsequently captured, tried for heresy and burned at the stake in the city of Rouen England on May 30, 1431, --- when she was just 19. Then, a half millennium following her execution, the Church reversed its position and made her a saint in 1920. No one is more symbolic of courageous women than is Joan of Arc.

In contrast to Joan of Arc stands Florence Nightingale. Nightingale, who became a beloved symbol for her work in providing healing for those wounded in battle, was born in 1820. Her affluent and socially connected British parents were living in Florence Italy at the time of her arrival. And despite her privileged childhood, she began showing a strong sense of compassion for the poor and afflicted at a very early age. She would go on to defy her parents demand that she wed someone of means and she refused a “suitable” proposal so that she could devote her life to nursing. Foremost among her many endeavors were her achievements during the Crimean War in the 1850s. Nightingale and her team of 34 nurses, upon arriving in the Turkish city of Constantinople, started immediately to correct the filthy conditions at a British base hospital. And, by purging the hospital of the disease spawning grime, they swiftly reduced the death count by an astonishing two-thirds among the wounded patients. Thus, in the squalor of that wartime hospital, Florence Nightingale and her team of nurses forever changed the unwholesome practices that had caused so many avoidable deaths among battlefield survivors throughout the ages.

Florence Nightingale spent her nights making the rounds and giving personal care to the wounded in that infirmary. And she became known to those who did not know her by name as the “LADY with the Lamp.” Florence Nightingale lived a life that was defined by restoring war torn human beings and mending those who were hurt wherever she found them. And before her life was to reach the end of its earthly duration in the year of 1910, when she was 90, this remarkable woman had dutifully established the St. Thomas Hospital and the Nightingale Training School for Nurses in the city of London.


America’s Women Warriors

From its inception the United States has been blessed with women who have chosen to stand beside their men as they fought to defend our nation. During the Revolutionary War, a woman named Mary Ludwig Hays fought alongside her husband during the 1778 battle at Monmouth. During that hot June day with musket-balls saturating the air around her, Mary ran tirelessly among the troops to deliver refreshing pitchers of water. And, for that action, she became known as Molly Pitcher. It is said that her husband died from the heat or that he was killed during those hours of fighting. Molly then took over his job as matross, someone who loads and fires cannons, and she continued firing away at the Redcoats. Another name that is listed as the inspiration for the legend of Molly Pitcher is that of Margaret Corbin. Corbin, in much the same manner as Mary Hays, had delivered pitchers of water and then manned a cannon gun following the death of her husband during the battle of Ft. Washington in 1776.


African American Women in the Military

A little known circumstance of the Revolutionary War was the role played by black women who were slaves on the plantations. Many of those slave women acted as cooks and nurses for the wounded soldiers and they took excellent care of the slave owners’ children when wives would leave home for extended periods to visit their husbands. However, among the more important contributions by those African-American slave women was the information they would gather as spies against the British Army.

African American women continued to serve as cooks and nurses for both Confederate and Union soldiers during the Civil War. And we must never forget the inspirational influence of Harriet Tubman who served as a Union spy, an unpaid soldier, a freedom fighter and a volunteer nurse during that gory conflict between the states.

Incidentally, an interesting history of the African American woman’s role in Americas’ wars can be found on the website, www.buffalosoldiersresearchmuseum.org.


Modern Day Women Warriors

Women continued to be involved in our nations’ wars, primarily as cooks, nurses and care-givers up until WW-II when their roles were expanded and they were allowed to put on the uniforms of the four military branches. Women then took on the roles of chauffeurs, clerks and other non-combat roles in all branches of the services. And it was a young WAC corporal, Helen Kogel-Denton, now deceased, who single-handed typed the reams of orders for the D-Day Invasion that was to be issued by General Eisenhower. Helen Denton, originally from South Dakota, went on to work for Delta Airlines as a secretary in the maintenance department and for the many years preceding her death, she lived in the suburbs south of Atlanta.

Following WW-II, in 1948, President Truman signed a law that made women a permanent part of the military services. However, it was not until 1976 when a small group of women were admitted into a U. S. military academy. Since then the rolls of women in the five branches of the military have been gradually expanded to where women are now involved in every aspect of the U. S. military. However, it was not until 2013 that the Army issued an order to end the policy of “no women in units that are tasked with direct combat.”

From the Mollie Pitchers of the American Revolution and up until the present day in contemporary America, women have played a vital role and have performed brilliantly both as nurses and on the field of battle. From providing refreshing containers of water to the soldiers in Gen. George Washington’s Army, women have broken through the barriers and they now fly helicopters, fighter jets, transport planes and bombers. And, in the Coast Guard, they command ships and vessels. In recent years women have attained the lofty ranks of admiral and general. At some point in time, a woman will become either Chairman of the Joint Chiefs or be appointed Chief of Staff for one or more of the services.

As of 2011 it was reported that some 214,098 women were serving on active duty and that they constituted 14.6% of the total active duty force. An additional 72,290 females were numbered among the Reserve and Guard categories and they represented 15.5% of the reserve forces. The Air Force has the highest percentage of women with 19.1% on active duty and 26.6% in the reserves. The Marine Corps fields the fewest women with only 6.8% of those who serve being on active duty and only 5.7% being in the Marine Corps Reserve.
