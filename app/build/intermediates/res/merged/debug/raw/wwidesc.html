WW-I began in June 1914 and the ensuing battles were fought on the worlds’ great oceans and most of the continents. Hostilities would last some four and a half years before the Armistice was signed on November 11, 1918, a date that we now celebrate as Veteran’s Day.

The conflict was called the War to End All Wars or simply, the Great War. And, even though some thirty nations mustered over sixty five million men to fight those century ago battles, WW-I was waged primarily among the nations of Europe. Casualties were enormous and a total of thirty five million soldiers and civilians were killed, captured or wounded.

Of the ten million deaths experienced by the military forces, two thirds of that number was inflicted on the fields of battle. However, at least one third of the military deaths resulted from the Spanish flu pandemic of 1918-19. Noncombatant deaths totaled more than five million and millions of additional noncombatants are listed among the 20 million soldiers and civilians who were wounded. In prior wars, the majority of military deaths were from disease and not from the insane clashes that sent men marching into the blazing muskets of an entrenched enemy.

Russia activated twelve million men and the Russian contingent was the largest force to fight on either side during the Great War. The czarist soldiers endured a casualty rate of almost 76% with some 9.1 million members of that giant militia killed, wounded, taken prisoner or declared missing.

Following Russia, France sustained the second highest Allied losses with casualties topping out at over 73% of its 8.4 million Man military apparatus.

For the Central Powers, Austria-Hungary assembled some 7.8 million soldiers and a staggering 90% of that number became casualties. Germany also endured heavy losses and nearly 65% of its eleven million man army and navy became victims of the fighting.

United States Becomes Involved

The U. S. did not enter the war until the final eighteen months of hostilities. During that time, American casualties, though moderate by comparison to other nations, exceeded 7.0% of the 4.5 million individuals who answered the call to serve. Some 116,000 U. S. soldiers died in battle and another 204,000 U. S. service members were wounded. And, an additional 4,500 of the so-called American “doughboys” were captured or went missing.

Native Americans Contribute

Even though Native Americans were compelled to wait until 1924 to become citizens and be accorded the full rights of citizenship, nearly 13,000 of those brave men went off to fight in WW-I. Native Americans made important battlefield contributions and some two decades preceding the Navajo Code Talkers of WW-II, Choctaw tribesmen introduced the concept of transmitting messages in their native dialect. --- German intelligence could not interpret the complex language and the Choctaw Code Talkers, members of Oklahoma’s National Guard, provided an immeasurable service for their combat units in WW-I.

African American Soldiers

Over 200,000 African Americans served during the Great War but only 11% of those men were assigned to fighting divisions. Most were dispersed to segregated regiments and used to load cargo, build roads and dig ditches. Minimal numbers of black soldiers were allowed to join the fighting units. However, those who saw combat gave an impressive account of themselves. The Harlem Hell Fighters, an all-black unit, fought with courage and determination. Their daring exploits went largely unnoticed in the United States. But the bravery shown by those men resulted in that unit being awarded the French Croix de Guerre, a prestigious medal that was conferred on allied soldiers and units that fought with valor by a grateful French government.

A bit of upbeat irony involves a member of the Harlem Hell Fighters some ninety seven years following his heroic actions on the night of May 15, 1918. Even though it took nearly a century for Private Henry Johnson, a diminutive black soldier to receive the Medal of Honor, it was on June 2, 2015 that President Barak Obama bestowed our nation’s highest military honor on that deserving soldier. A second Medal of Honor was awarded during that same White House ceremony to another deserving minority soldier of WW-I. He was Jewish Sgt. William Shemin, also from New York. Thus, some nine decades following their heroic actions, two ethnic minority soldiers received posthumously the recognition that had been denied them almost a century earlier by our country’s first minority president.

Many thousands of African-American civilians also made important contributions to the war effort. They migrated from small farms and villages across the states of Dixie to live and work in the industrialized cities of the north. In cities like Detroit, Gary, Pittsburg, Akron and Cleveland, Afro-Americans took on the labors of heavy industry. They kept vital war materials flowing to the former factory workers who had become soldiers and were fighting on the battle fronts of Europe.

Supplies and Weapons

In addition to its commitment of military service personnel, the U. S. shipped over 7.5 million tons of war materials across the Atlantic in support of the Allied effort. Listed among those supplies were over 70,000 mules and horses, 50,000 trucks, 27,000 freight cars and over 1,800 steam locomotives.

Tanks and to some extent submarines were used for the first time as weapons during

WW-I, and the methods of aerial warfare were birthed over those battlefields when fearless pilots engaged in plane-versus-plane combat. During those years of struggle, 274 German U-boats went forth to prowl the seas and wreak havoc on allied shipping. Those submarines torpedoed some 6,596 allied vessels with a heavy loss of life and untold tons of supplies and equipment being lost to those encounters. Most of the cargo ships were torpedoed near the shorelines of Europe and in the stormy waters of the English Channel.

The introduction of air power exposed innumerable thousands’ of behind the lines civilians to the horrors of conflict. And with the arrival of aerial warfare came the phrase, dogfight. The expression came from pilots shutting down their engines to prevent stalling following a sudden maneuver during air to air combat. And then, as the engine struggled to reignite, it would cough numerous times and emit sounds that were remindful of barking dogs.

Poisonous Gas

With the introduction of chlorine gas it became clear that the horse cavalry was finished as a means of combat. Tanks would be used to replace the cavalry units and during the two decades separating the world wars, the early tank would be upgraded to become a premier weapon with which to engage in ground warfare. --- Hitler would then use the technologically improved tank and the more sophisticated versions of WW-I airplanes to launch the terrifying Nazi blitzkriegs of WW-II. With tanks firing away and rumbling across the ground, attended by siren shrieking dive bombers plunging from the skies overhead, the Nazis’ launched their invasions.

It is arguable as to whether it was Germany or France that first employed poisonous gas as a weapon of mass destruction. Nonetheless, before World War-I would come to its conclusion, Germany would have released some 68,000 tons of the toxic substance with an addition 51,000 tons being discharged by Great Britain and France. Some 1.2 million soldiers from both sides suffered the effects of the poisoned air and over 91,000 of those men died excruciating deaths.

Noteworthy Trivia

Detailed events of WW-I are far too complex and many to cover during this brief narrative. However, several circumstances of that war deserve some mention.

  1. The Selective Service was first introduced by the United States during WW-I. And, the so-called draft was implemented as a means to secure the men needed to serve in the various branches of the military.

  2. The phrase, posttraumatic stress disorder, PTSD, was first described as shellshock or battle fatigue during WW-I. Those descriptive terms continued in use during WW-II, Korea and even into the early years of Vietnam.

  3. A defining report on the Great War was the Battle of Somme. That struggle produced the greatest single day loss of life in the long history of the British Army. Royal Army casualties during that one day of combat totaled more than 60,000.

Unusual Events on the Battlefield

Finally, the most unlikely event of WW-I occurred on Christmas Eve, 1914. Opposing British and German soldiers spontaneously emerged from their miserable trenches to sing Christmas Carols for each other. Then, on Christmas Day, the troops from both sides along two thirds of the battlefront declared a truce, an unofficial respite from the fighting that in some sectors lasted an entire week. ------ The following year, sentries on both sides were ordered to shoot anyone who attempted to repeat the previous years’ performance.
