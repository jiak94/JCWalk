Among the more horrific conditions to which any service member will ever be subjected is to be taken Prisoner of War, or to go Missing in Action. From the decadence of the Civil War camps in Andersonville and Elmira to the Bataan Death March during WW-II, and on to the fetid conditions of the camps in Japan, Germany, Russia, Korea, Vietnam and locations in the current areas of conflict, the atrocious treatment of captured soldiers defies all perceptions of human decency. And the mortality rate among those held captive is staggering. Thus, to be taken POW is oft times a fate that is worse than death.

The Bataan Death March

Following the fall of Corregidor during the early days of WW-II, --- the 72,000 U. S. and Philippine solders that were captured, many of them already sick and starving, were sent on a sixty three mile march to where they were to be held at Camp O’Donnell. Many died during the five day trek from a lack of food and water in addition to those that were executed by the brutal Japanese guards. Upon arrival at that destination, --- it was discovered that some 7,000 to 10,000 of those subdued soldiers had died while in-route. The POWs were given no food and little or no water during the March of Death. And even though there were artesian wells flowing with the life sustaining liquid scattered along the way, the Japanese guards would shoot anyone caught attempting to snitch a drink.

Many who survived the hike were sent to prison camps in Japan where the treatment was merciless. And, from among the 140,000 POWS held in those camps, one of every three died from starvation, the horrendous work-load, disease or the corporal punishment doled-out by the guards. The feudal mind-set of the Japanese dictated that they fight until death and they looked with contempt upon anyone who showed the immoral weakness to surrender.

In contrast to the Japanese, the American custom was to treat POWs in a humane manner.

Therefore, U. S. held prisoners were never tortured or starved and their medical needs received attention. Both the Germans and the Soviets rivaled the Japanese in the viciousness with which they treated those who were captured. Even so, no depth of depravity can ever match or surpass the evil treatment that became the lot of the unfortunate wretches who were rounded up and murdered by the millions in the concentration camps of the Nazis.

Germany is estimated to have taken from 4.0 to 5.7 million Russian POWs. And it is reckoned that some 2.7 to 3.3 million of those Soviet soldiers died while in German custody.

Those calculations reflect a death rate among the Soviet captives of an astounding 68%.

The number of Germans captured by the USSR is thought to be over 3.1 million. And the deaths among those Russian held Nazi soldiers are estimated to range from 475,000 to nearly 1.01 million. However, the whereabouts of 1.3 million German soldiers remain unknown.

Korea, Vietnam and the Middle East

American POWs were again subjected to sadistic brutality in Korea and the fate of thousands of those American service members is still unknown. Most of those missing are no doubt dead. But, many among them could have suffered the still unknown fate of those who were confirmed to be Missing in Action. --- Needless to say, the brutality in the North Korean prison camps rivaled that of the Japanese during WW-II, --- in every probable way!

During Vietnam a satanic ritual of cruelty again became the order of the day for captive Americans! Men like former Navy pilots McCain, Denton, Stockdale, Mulligan and Alvarez were to become the symbolic faces of those who endured the hellish torture at North Vietnam’s so-called Hanoi Hilton. And, even lesser known detainees were subjected to the same measures of incredible cruelty. Because of their years of torture in the prison camps of Vietnam, everyone grasped the depth of feeling when Admiral Jeremiah Denton stepped from the plane returning the POWs to the Philippines, and then salute the flag and utter the phrase, “God Bless America.” With those three simple words an emotional Admiral Denton communicated his pride in what it means to be an American! And with that straightforward and guileless utterance, --- the admiral brought forth tears of joy to millions of his fellow Americans.

With the dawning of the 21st Century a war-weary world shifted into a new era of warfare, an era during which there will be stunning advances in technology and weaponry. However, the fanatical enemies we now oppose have the same cruel mindset that has caused so much pain and suffering among those held prisoner throughout history.

Ours is a radical enemy that will look with disdain on those captured. And, from North Africa to the Middle East and perhaps even within the U. S., we confront a radical enemy that will slit the throats and sever the heads of any with whom they disagree, even non-combatant women and innocent children.

Thus, as veterans of past conflicts, it is our hope that none who serve will ever know the horror of being taken prisoner or listed among the missing. And we pray that an almighty Providence will have mercy on any who might be taken during our nations’ hi-tech battles of the future. Advanced weaponry has diminished the risk of being captured but it has in no way diminished the horrific treatment that will be accorded those taken prisoner in the new century.
